Introduction

2nd year PhD Public Health Genetics ( data science/ml option ) at the University of Washington, Seattle, WA.

Completing PhD with the McCrone Lab , Bedford Lab and Nextstrain jointly mentored by Dr. JT McCrone and Dr. Trevor Bedford.**

Working at the nexus of computional virology + genomic epidemiology + AI/LLMs. For an overview of the field - see Trevor's terrific explanation →here

Focusing on viral dynamics across biological scales from molecular to global phylodynamics linking within-host viral evolution to between-host viral dynamics to inform vaccines/MCMs, viral emergence and new fundamental virology. Also interested in AI-driven predictive models such as genomic Large Language Models (LLMs) to predict viral-host compatibility and enhance Nextstrain's real-time tracking of pathogen evolution.

Engaging in high-level strategic discussions on global pandemic preparedness and biosecurity as part of the Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security - Emerging Leaders in Biosecurity (ELBI) Fellowship, Class of 2024. Engaged in key sessions in Washington, DC →here with the White House — Office of Pandemic Preparedness and Response Policy (OPPR), led by Major General (Ret.) Paul Friedrichs. Further contributions took place in Geneva, Switzerland →here at the World Health Organization and the United Nations Institute for Disarmament Research. A key highlight was taking on the role of National Institutes of Health (NIH) Director to provide strategic oversight in coordinating the nation's biomedical research response, guiding efforts to accelerate development of vaccines and medical countermeasures (MCMs) in a tabletop simulation exercise based on Clade X scenario.

Prior, I formally trained as an epidemiologist holding a MS in Genetic Epidemiology and worked at the California Department of Public Health's Division of Communicable Disease Control in addition to completing a year-long field/applied epidemiology training program – UW Student Epidemic Action Leaders (SEAL) Team led by Dr. Janet Baseman through the UW Dept. of Epidemiology. Some of my research experience includes roles at the UW Center for Emerging and Re-emerging Infectious Disease Research in the Hawn Lab, the UW Institute for Protein Design in the Baker Lab***, the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, and Harvard's Center for Communicable Disease Dynamics.

Recent Publications (2021 - Present):
2025 (in preparation) Alveolar macrophage transcriptional signatures associated with resistance to TST/IGRA conversion following Mycobacterium tuberculosis exposure
Haynes, A. M., Øvadiuc, C., Peterson, G. J., Worodria, W., Nalukwago, S., Muwanguzi, T., Nsereko, M., Stein, C. M., Dill-MacFarland, K., Mayanja-Kizza, H., Boom, W. H., Segnitz, M., & Hawn, T. R.

Importance: This study investigates transcriptional signatures in alveolar macrophages that may confer natural resistance to tuberculosis infection, providing insights into novel mechanisms of disease resistance and potential therapeutic targets.
2025 (in review at AJHG) Insights on improving accessibility and usability of functional data to unlock its potential for variant interpretation
Park, M. S., Kumar, R. D., Øvadiuc, C., Folta, A., McEwen, A. E., Snyder, A., ... & Stergachis, A. B. (2025). medRxiv, 2025-01.

Importance: This survey of 190 genetics professionals revealed critical gaps in the use of functional data for clinical variant interpretation. The findings highlight the urgent need for a comprehensive database with reliable quality metrics and standardized guidelines for handling conflicting functional evidence in variant classification. See the survey here.
2023 Biological Signatures of Infectious Disease Resistance: Deciphering immunological mechanisms of resistance to tuberculosis (TB) by combining host genetics and immuno-transcriptomics
Available from Dissertations & Theses @ University of Washington WCLP; ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global 2023

Importance: By identifying population outliers with rare human genetic mutations displaying infectious disease resistance, sequencing their genomes, and exploring the mechanisms behind their unique traits, this thesis shows an example of how we can discover new drug/vaccine targets focusing on adaptive and protective rare human genetic variation.

Long-term Research Aims:

:: Reverse-engineering viral evolution / fitness landscapes mapping a path towards shifting from reactive to anticipatory defense to preempt and counter emerging high-consequence bio-threats. (see this paper and wikipedia entries on fitness landscapes and viral swarms/quasispecies)

:: While AI advances accelerate the reverse-engineering of viral evolution, they simultaneously create an urgent dual-use risk by lowering barriers to engineer novel threat - the true extent of which requires immediate assessment.

:: This requires a phased approach w/ (A) RED TEAMING AI Models for vulnerability mapping, (B) development of AI-specific biothreat models and technical safeguards, and (C) informed policy development based on mitigation assessments and expert engagement. National Academies of Science workshop/publication on Assessing and Navigating Biosecurity Concerns and Benefits of Artificial Intelligence Use in the Life Sciences provides additional context.
